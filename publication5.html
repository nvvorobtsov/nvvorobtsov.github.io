Telescopic Properties of Ramanujan Polynomials and Their Connection to Prime Number Distribution Modulo 6

N. Vorobtsov • 2026
DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.19238858

Theoretical Foundations

This research reveals a profound, previously unnoticed modular symmetry within Srinivasa Ramanujan's identity for the sum of cubes. The identity is governed by four base polynomials:

\[ W(a, b) = a^7 - 3a^4b + a(3b^2 - 1) \] \[ X(a, b) = a^7 - 3a^4(1 + b) + a(2 + 6b + 3b^2) \] \[ Y(a, b) = 2a^6 - 3a^3(1 + 2b) + 1 + 3b + 3b^2 \] \[ Z(a, b) = a^6 - 1 - 3b - 3b^2 \]

These satisfy the classic identity: \( X^3 + Y^3 + Z^3 = W^3 \).

The generative power of this identity lies in its telescopic shift conditions, which allow massive chains of cubes to collapse algebraically:

\[ X(a, b) = W(a, b+1) \] \[ Z(a, b) = -Y(a, b+a^3) \]

The Prime Number Connection (Modulo 6)

By fixing the scaling parameter \( a = 6 \), we uncover a strict alignment with the fundamental prime number ranges \( 6n \pm 1 \):

\[ X(6, b) \equiv 0 \pmod 6 \] \[ W(6, b) \equiv 0 \pmod 6 \] \[ Y(6, b) \equiv 1 \pmod 6 \quad \longleftarrow \text{Right prime range } (6n+1) \] \[ Z(6, b) \equiv -1 \pmod 6 \quad \longleftarrow \text{Left prime range } (6n-1) \]

This proves that Ramanujan's identity acts as a perfect algebraic balance between the two fundamental classes of prime numbers: \( 0^3 + 1^3 + (-1)^3 \equiv 0 \pmod 6 \).

Massive Cube Decompositions

By leveraging the telescopic nature of the polynomials, we can generate arbitrarily long, exact decompositions of cubes. Below are two striking examples of this mechanism in action.

Generation Example 1: The 8-Cube Chain

Setting the base scale \( a=2 \), starting point \( b_0=10 \), and telescope steps \( k=5 \). After moving negative terms and canceling common cubes, we obtain a pure decomposition where the sum of two cubes equals the sum of eight unique cubes.

633 + 7563 = 33 + 453 + 2463 + 2673 + 3333 + 4053 + 4833 + 5673

Sum = 432,331,263 (Factorization: \( 3^6 \cdot 7^4 \cdot 13 \cdot 19 \))

Generation Example 3: The 31-Cube Monolith

By increasing the scale to \( a=3 \), starting at \( b_0=-16 \), and running the telescope for \( k=15 \) steps, the left side collapses to a single cube, while the right side expands into a massive sum of 31 unique cubes.

83763 = 83 + 983 + 1823 + 2603 + 3323 + 3983 + 4583 + 5123 + 5603 + 6023 + 6383 + 6683 + 6923 + 7103 + 7223 + 17083 + 18823 + 20623 + 22483 + 24363 + 24403 + 26383 + 28423 + 30523 + 32683 + 34903 + 37183 + 39523 + 41923 + 44383 + 46903

Sum = 587,638,181,376 (Factorization: \( 2^9 \cdot 3^3 \cdot 349^3 \))
